>> Forge Squad

During my Master's in Game Development and Simulation, my team - "Wizard Dudes" - won the Best in Class Award in 2020 for Forge Squad, a game we developed using Unreal Engine 4. I was the team leader and music/SFX lead, and wrote the logic for many of the game's crafting-related elements and tools. I also contributed to enemy wave generation scripts, spawning logic, and large-scale refactoring. Together, we made the game ready for release, and published it on Steam.


>> Lunatrix

After the success of Forge Squad, my team and I participated in the 48 hour Global Game Jam 2022 and created Lunatrix, a game based on the theme "Duality" that challenges players to defend a growing base from attacking spaceships. I designed the building grid system and the algorithm to rotate tetris-style modular base expansions and combine them into groups that merged together similar buildings as they were added to the base.


>> More games!

Throughout my career, I have developed a variety of solo projects under tight time constraints, ranging from 48 to 72 hours, such as the following:

"Soul Eater" (Ludum Dare) is a story-based, ASCII-only, turn-based RPG that incorporates strategic decision-making in balancing character stamina and HP by consuming the soul energy of defeated enemies or items. Stronger enemies could call for help, adding complexity to the game.

"Ballistix" (LD 27) is a physics-based catapult game that challenges players to feed vegetarian plants with their desired fruit and sell well-fed plants for a profit. The soundtrack is randomly generated from song fragments I created to fit together, resulting in a looping music experience with many possible combinations. [>> Link]

"Solar System Hero" (LD 30) requires players to establish trade routes between planets orbiting a sun. Each planet's layout indicates demand/surplus of resources, and automated ships must be sent to collect and drop off specific resources. Buildings on each planet provide additional demand and surplus resources. [>> Link]

"CPU Fight" (high-school) allows combatants to add and upgrade PC parts and use "hacking" commands to annoy and kick out adversaries from the game.

The list goes on with various genres, including 3-lane-runner-with-fire-extinguisher, top-down-2d-shooter-with-mining-and-3d-rendered-models, turn-based-time-travel-mystery, racing and jump 'n' run.

>> Humble beginnings

Ever since I played video games, I wanted to know how to make them. Without having access to internet at an early age, I gave up for a while, after the built-in BASIC tutorial of my dad's x286 laptop told me to use "PRINT", but that button labeled "Print Scn" just wouldn't execute the command.

In highschool, I finally had internet access. I would deliberately stay in school longer to learn more about ActionScript 3, the only language I was taught at the time. When our professor challenged us to write a Flash game within 2 hours, where we had to draw a crosshair, and increment the score on each clicked target that would then randomly jump to a different location on screen - I proudly presented my solution after 10 minutes and was allowed to teach the class for the rest of the day and help them complete their projects.

That's when I resumed my plan from primary school; I knew what I wanted to be doing for my life - "something with computers". Years later, I ended up as a software engineer, but I kept writing games and music ever since.
